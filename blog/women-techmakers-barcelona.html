Women Techmakers Barcelona: Networking, Mentoring, and Reflections on Gender in Tech

Women Techmakers Barcelona · March 2026 · Barcelona, Spain

About the event

Women Techmakers Barcelona is a community event dedicated to providing visibility, community, and resources for women in technology. This year's edition centred on the theme Break the Pattern — a call to challenge the status quo in how technology is built and by whom.

The event brought together professionals, researchers, and students across a range of disciplines, with a programme of talks, networking spaces, and one-to-one mentoring sessions. It was held in Barcelona and co-organised with the International Women's Day (IWD) initiative.

What I attended

The programme featured three talks, each approaching the intersection of technology and gender from a different angle:

  • Architecting Production Agentic Systems: Stop Treating Agents Like APIs — Christian Esther Enriquez. A technical deep-dive into what it actually means to build agentic AI systems at production scale. The talk challenged the common tendency to treat agents as simple API wrappers and instead made the case for designing systems that can plan, delegate, and handle uncertainty — while keeping humans in a meaningful position of control.
  • Vibe-Coding a MVP Women's Health App in 48 Hours with AI Tools and Strategic Scope — Maria Jose Lalama. A hands-on account of building a minimum viable product for a women's health application using AI-assisted development tools within a 48-hour constraint. The talk was as much about scoping and decision-making under pressure as it was about the tools themselves.
  • Predict the Gender Pay Gap with Machine Learning — Anaïs Pepey. A data-driven talk exploring how machine learning can be applied to model and predict gender pay disparities. It raised important questions about what the data can and cannot tell us, and what structural factors sit beyond the reach of any model.

Beyond the formal sessions, a significant part of the day was spent networking — meeting people working in data science, product, engineering, and UX. It was a genuinely mixed crowd, and the conversations were as valuable as the talks themselves.

1:1 mentoring sessions

One of the highlights of the day was the structured 1:1 mentoring programme. I had two sessions: one with a software engineer and one with a project manager, both working in the tech industry.

Both conversations helped me understand how my background — a PhD combining biophysics, proteomics, and computational image analysis — maps onto roles in the tech sector. The software engineer helped me think through where technical depth matters most and how to frame a research background for engineering teams. The project manager offered a complementary perspective: the value of scientific rigour in structuring problems, managing uncertainty, and communicating clearly across disciplines.

What I found most useful was the specificity of their advice. Rather than generic career guidance, both mentors engaged with my actual experience and helped me see which parts of it transfer most directly — and which might need to be translated or contextualised for a non-academic audience.

Reflections

Leaving the event, three things stayed with me:

  • There are real opportunities for women in tech right now. The demand for people who can work at the intersection of complex domains — data, science, communication, product — is high. Events like this make that tangible, not just aspirational.
  • The gender imbalance is still significant. Despite the progress represented by an event like this, the gap in representation — particularly in senior technical roles — is real and well-documented. Naming it directly, as several speakers did, felt more honest than treating it as a problem nearly solved.
  • Scientific and technical literacy is rarer than it should be. Multiple conversations touched on this: many people entering tech lack the foundational scientific or quantitative training to engage critically with the tools and systems they are building or managing. This is a genuine gap — and one that people with research backgrounds are well-placed to help address.

Attending this event was a reminder that community matters — and that there is a growing, serious conversation happening at the intersection of gender, science, and technology that I want to stay connected to.
